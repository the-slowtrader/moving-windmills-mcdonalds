"I Can Be a Curious Inventor"

This is the book your child brought home. It's based on a true story — a boy named William, in Malawi, who built a working windmill out of scrap metal when he was 14.

He's grown up now. He came back to his village and built something bigger.

Here's what happened next.

William's Story

Young William / Malawi landscape

In 2001, a famine hit Malawi.

William Kamkwamba was 14. His family couldn't afford school fees. He had to drop out.

Library / "Using Energy" textbook

But he kept going to the village library.

He found a textbook called "Using Energy" — written in English, a language he couldn't fully read. From one photograph of a windmill, he taught himself how to build one.

The original windmill

Using scrap metal, bicycle parts, and tractor components, he built a windmill that powered his family's home.

His neighbors called him crazy. Then the lights came on.

Grace House compound — modern

That was 25 years ago.

Today, William runs Moving Windmills Project in Kasungu, Malawi. Young people come to learn engineering, solar technology, and farming. Over 200 have come through so far.

The windmill was just the beginning.

Grace House is where it happens. Laptops, mentorship, solar engineering, sustainable farming — all under one roof in Kasungu, Malawi.

It's not a classroom. Kids and young adults just show up. The door is always open.
